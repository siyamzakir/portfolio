April 2014. I landed my first Upwork contract — a WordPress site for a small business in the US. I was paid $120 for something that took me a week. I thought I was doing great.

Ten years later I'm CTO of [AppsZone](https://appszonebd.com), have shipped projects across four continents, and still pick up the occasional Upwork contract because I genuinely enjoy the work.

Here's what a decade of remote freelancing actually teaches you.

## 1. Scope creep kills more projects than bad code

The $120 WordPress site became a $300 project because the client kept adding features and I didn't know how to say "that's a new scope." Protect your scope in writing, always. A simple email: _"Happy to add this — it'll be X hours at the agreed rate. Shall I proceed?"_ prevents 90% of disputes.

## 2. Communication frequency > communication quality

Early on I would disappear for three days then send a beautifully written 500-word update. Clients hated it. Now I send a two-sentence update every morning. Short. Specific. No surprises.

> "Working on the lead qualification logic today. Will share a demo link by EOD."

That's it. Clients feel in control. You feel less anxious about being judged.

## 3. Async is a superpower if you set boundaries

Working across timezones with clients in Australia, the US, and Europe from Dhaka means most communication is async. This is actually a feature — you get deep work blocks without meeting interruptions. The discipline: respond to messages within 24 hours. Don't let async become a ghost.

## 4. Charge for what you know, not just what you do

The hours I spent learning Docker, Kubernetes, and cloud-native deployments weren't billed to clients — but they directly enabled me to charge $80/hr instead of $25/hr. Skill investment compounds. Always be learning something clients haven't asked for yet.

## 5. Never work without a contract. Never.

Three horror stories in year one taught me this permanently. Even a one-page email agreement with: scope, payment terms, revision rounds, and kill fee. If a client won't sign, they're not a client.

## 6. Your GitHub is your real portfolio

I've had clients hire me off [my GitHub profile](https://github.com/siyamzakir) without asking for references. Code is public proof. Write clean commits, document your work, contribute to things. The profile compounds over time.

## 7. Specialisation earns more than generalisation

When I was "a web developer," my rate was low. When I became "a Laravel developer who specialises in complex CRMs," my rate tripled. Counterintuitively, narrowing your pitch widens your client pool — because the right clients find you instead of you competing with everyone.

## 8. Build systems, not just features

The best freelance projects I delivered weren't the ones with the most features — they were the ones I could hand off with clean documentation, reproducible deployments, and a test suite. Clients who see real engineering come back. Clients who see hacked-together code don't.

## 9. Automate the admin or it eats you

Invoicing, time tracking, follow-up emails — all automatable. I use a simple GitHub Actions + Notion workflow to track project milestones and bill on completion. The mental overhead of manual admin is real.

## 10. The best clients are the ones who trust you

The immigration CRM for Future Connect in Australia ([fccrm.futureconnect.net.au](https://fccrm.futureconnect.net.au)) started as a small Upwork contract. They gave me the autonomy to architect it properly. It became a multi-year engagement. Cultivate those relationships. Protect them fiercely.
